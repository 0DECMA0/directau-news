The tragic events surrounding the Clune family last month have ignited a raw conversation regarding the lack of support for Australians managing severe disability. Simon Lewis, whose daughter lives with profound autism, argues that the broader narrative has been "hijacked," framing the condition as a mere personality quirk rather than the debilitating medical reality faced by those at the severe end of the spectrum.

Speaking from a place of shared experience, Mr Lewis highlights the immense strain placed on caregivers who operate within a system he describes as failing to address high-needs cases. He warns that the romanticisation of neurodiversity in popular media creates a dangerous disconnect, leaving those dealing with non-verbal or violent manifestations of the disorder isolated and without adequate intervention from the state.

> "When a medical crisis is rebranded as a social identity, we risk erasing the visible struggle of families pushed to their absolute breaking point."

As the community continues to process the recent loss of the Clune family, advocates are calling for an urgent review of the NDIS and local support frameworks. The objective is to ensure that the distinction between mild neurodivergence and severe disability is formally recognised, providing a vital lifeline to those who currently feel they are drowning in plain sight.
